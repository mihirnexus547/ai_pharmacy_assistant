OVERVIEW

Vitamin C, also known as ascorbic acid, is a water soluble vitamin that is essential for normal growth, tissue repair, immune function, and overall health. Unlike many animals, humans cannot produce vitamin C naturally, making dietary intake or supplementation necessary to maintain adequate levels. Vitamin C belongs to the class of essential vitamins and functions as a powerful antioxidant that helps protect cells from damage caused by free radicals. It plays a critical role in the synthesis of collagen, which is a structural protein required for healthy skin, blood vessels, bones, cartilage, tendons, ligaments, and wound healing. Vitamin C also enhances the absorption of non heme iron from plant based foods by converting iron into a form that is more easily absorbed in the digestive tract. This property makes it especially important for individuals at risk of iron deficiency anemia. The vitamin supports normal immune system function by promoting the activity of white blood cells and strengthening the body's natural defense mechanisms against infections. In addition, vitamin C contributes to the production of several neurotransmitters, including norepinephrine, and is involved in the metabolism of proteins and certain hormones. As an antioxidant, it helps regenerate other antioxidants such as vitamin E, thereby contributing to overall cellular protection. Vitamin C is widely available in fruits and vegetables such as oranges, strawberries, kiwifruit, bell peppers, broccoli, and tomatoes, but supplements are commonly used when dietary intake is inadequate. The body stores only limited amounts of vitamin C, and excess quantities are usually excreted through the urine because of its water soluble nature. Vitamin C deficiency can lead to scurvy, a condition characterized by fatigue, gum disease, poor wound healing, joint pain, bruising, and anemia. Maintaining adequate vitamin C intake is essential for optimal physiological function, immune health, and connective tissue integrity.

INDICATIONS & USES

Vitamin C is primarily indicated for the prevention and treatment of vitamin C deficiency, including the treatment of scurvy. It is prescribed for individuals whose dietary intake is insufficient or whose medical conditions increase the body's need for vitamin C. Patients with malnutrition, alcoholism, eating disorders, gastrointestinal disorders that impair nutrient absorption, or those undergoing dialysis may require supplementation to prevent deficiency. Vitamin C is also used to improve iron absorption in patients with iron deficiency anemia, particularly when iron supplements alone are insufficient. It is commonly included as part of nutritional support in hospitalized patients, individuals recovering from surgery, severe burns, or traumatic injuries because collagen synthesis is essential for tissue repair. The United States Food and Drug Administration recognizes vitamin C supplementation for the prevention and treatment of vitamin C deficiency. Healthcare providers may recommend vitamin C supplementation during pregnancy or lactation when dietary intake does not meet nutritional requirements, although supplementation should remain within recommended daily limits unless otherwise directed. Vitamin C is frequently used off label to support immune function during respiratory infections such as the common cold, although evidence suggests that while regular supplementation may slightly reduce the duration of cold symptoms, it generally does not prevent infections in the general population. Some clinicians use vitamin C as an adjunctive therapy for wound healing, pressure ulcers, and chronic skin ulcers because of its role in collagen production. Vitamin C has also been studied as supportive therapy in cardiovascular disease, cataracts, and certain chronic inflammatory conditions, although current evidence remains inconclusive for routine clinical use in these settings. High dose intravenous vitamin C has been investigated in research settings for severe infections, sepsis, burns, and certain cancers, but these uses remain experimental and are not considered standard treatment. Overall, vitamin C supplementation is most beneficial when correcting deficiency or meeting increased physiological demands rather than treating unrelated diseases in individuals with normal vitamin C levels.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION

The dosage of vitamin C depends on the individual's age, nutritional status, underlying medical condition, and the reason for supplementation. For healthy adults, the recommended dietary allowance is generally 90 milligrams daily for men and 75 milligrams daily for women, with slightly higher requirements for smokers due to increased oxidative stress. For the prevention of vitamin C deficiency, supplements commonly provide between 100 and 500 milligrams daily, depending on dietary intake and clinical needs. In the treatment of scurvy, adults are often prescribed 100 to 300 milligrams daily for at least one month or until symptoms resolve, although dosing may vary based on clinical judgment. Higher oral doses, sometimes ranging from 500 to 2000 milligrams daily, are widely available over the counter, but routine high dose supplementation has not consistently demonstrated additional health benefits in healthy individuals. Vitamin C may be taken with or without food, although taking it with meals may reduce the likelihood of stomach discomfort in sensitive individuals. The supplement is available in tablets, chewable tablets, capsules, powders, liquid formulations, and intravenous preparations for specific medical situations. When large daily doses are prescribed, dividing the total amount into two or more doses throughout the day may improve absorption because intestinal uptake becomes less efficient at very high single doses. Adequate hydration is recommended, especially for individuals taking high doses, to help reduce the risk of kidney stone formation in susceptible patients. If a dose is missed, it should generally be taken as soon as remembered unless it is almost time for the next scheduled dose, in which case the missed dose should be skipped without doubling the next dose. Patients should avoid exceeding the tolerable upper intake level of 2000 milligrams per day without medical supervision because excessive intake may increase the risk of adverse effects. Individuals receiving intravenous vitamin C should receive treatment under the supervision of qualified healthcare professionals with appropriate monitoring.

SIDE EFFECTS

Vitamin C is generally well tolerated when taken within recommended doses, and most people experience few or no side effects. Common mild side effects include nausea, stomach discomfort, heartburn, abdominal cramps, bloating, gas, and diarrhea, particularly when high doses are consumed. Some individuals may notice a sour taste or mild gastrointestinal irritation when taking chewable or acidic formulations. These mild effects often improve by reducing the dose or taking the supplement with food. Moderate side effects may include persistent digestive upset, vomiting, headaches, flushing, or increased urinary frequency in susceptible individuals. Long term consumption of very high doses may increase urinary oxalate levels, which can contribute to kidney stone formation, particularly in people with a history of calcium oxalate stones. Excessive vitamin C intake may also interfere with certain laboratory tests, including blood glucose testing and stool occult blood testing, potentially leading to inaccurate results. Rare but serious side effects include severe allergic reactions characterized by rash, itching, swelling of the face, tongue, or throat, severe dizziness, and difficulty breathing. Individuals with glucose 6 phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency who receive extremely high intravenous doses may be at increased risk of hemolysis. Patients with hereditary iron overload disorders may accumulate excessive iron if taking large amounts of vitamin C over prolonged periods because of enhanced iron absorption. Very high intravenous doses have also been associated with kidney injury in susceptible individuals. Any severe abdominal pain, blood in the urine, persistent vomiting, signs of an allergic reaction, or symptoms suggestive of kidney stones should prompt immediate medical evaluation.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS

Vitamin C should be used according to recommended dietary guidelines or healthcare provider instructions, as excessive supplementation does not necessarily provide additional health benefits and may increase the risk of adverse effects. Individuals with a history of kidney stones, particularly calcium oxalate stones, should consult a healthcare provider before taking high dose vitamin C supplements because urinary oxalate levels may increase. Patients with significant kidney disease should use vitamin C cautiously, especially at doses above the recommended daily intake, since impaired kidney function may alter the handling of vitamin C metabolites. Individuals with hereditary hemochromatosis, thalassemia, or other iron overload disorders should avoid unnecessary high dose supplementation because vitamin C enhances iron absorption and may worsen iron accumulation. Patients with glucose 6 phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency should not receive very high intravenous doses unless specifically recommended and monitored by a healthcare specialist. Vitamin C is generally considered safe during pregnancy and breastfeeding when consumed within recommended dietary allowances, but very high doses should be avoided unless medically indicated because safety data for prolonged excessive intake are limited. There is no known harmful interaction between moderate alcohol consumption and vitamin C, although chronic alcohol use may contribute to vitamin C deficiency by reducing dietary intake and nutrient absorption. Vitamin C has relatively few clinically significant drug interactions, but it may affect the absorption or effectiveness of certain medications and may interfere with some laboratory tests, so patients should inform healthcare providers about all supplements they are taking. Individuals with liver disease generally do not require routine dose adjustments, but those with severe systemic illness should follow individualized medical advice. Vitamin C supplements should be stored properly and kept out of the reach of children to prevent accidental ingestion of excessive amounts. Emergency medical attention should be sought immediately if symptoms such as severe allergic reactions, difficulty breathing, swelling of the face or throat, severe chest pain, confusion, blood in the urine, severe flank pain, or other signs of a serious adverse reaction develop. Regular consultation with a healthcare professional is advisable for anyone planning long term high dose vitamin C supplementation, particularly if they have chronic medical conditions or take multiple medications.
