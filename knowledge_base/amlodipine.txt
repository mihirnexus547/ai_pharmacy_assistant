OVERVIEW:
Amlodipine is a widely prescribed medication belonging to the dihydropyridine class of calcium channel blockers. It functions by relaxing blood vessels throughout the body, which in turn lowers blood pressure and improves blood flow to the heart. This therapeutic effect is achieved through its biological mechanism of action, where it selectively inhibits the influx of calcium ions across cell membranes of vascular smooth muscle and cardiac muscle cells, primarily targeting L-type voltage-gated calcium channels. By blocking calcium entry, amlodipine causes peripheral vasodilation, reducing systemic vascular resistance and thus decreasing arterial blood pressure. Furthermore, it dilates coronary arteries and arterioles, both in normal and ischemic regions, thereby increasing myocardial oxygen delivery and reducing the workload on the heart. Its long-acting nature allows for convenient once-daily dosing, making it an effective and patient-friendly option for chronic management of cardiovascular conditions.

INDICATIONS & USES:
Amlodipine is primarily indicated for the treatment of several major cardiovascular conditions, with its efficacy well-established in clinical practice. Its official FDA-approved uses include the management of hypertension, commonly known as high blood pressure, in both adults and pediatric patients aged 6 years and older. Additionally, it is approved for the treatment of chronic stable angina, a type of chest pain caused by reduced blood flow to the heart, and vasospastic angina, also known as Prinzmetal's angina, which results from spasms of the coronary arteries. While primarily used for these approved indications, amlodipine may sometimes be considered off-label for conditions like Raynaud's phenomenon due to its vasodilatory properties, though other calcium channel blockers might be preferred for this specific use. Its main role remains firmly rooted in the effective control of blood pressure and the alleviation of angina symptoms.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION:
The standard adult dosage for amlodipine typically ranges from 2.5 mg to 10 mg, administered orally once daily. For most patients, treatment usually begins with an initial dose of 2.5 mg or 5 mg, which can then be adjusted based on the patient's response and tolerability, with a maximum recommended dose of 10 mg per day. In pediatric patients aged 6 to 17 years with hypertension, the recommended starting dose is 2.5 mg once daily, with a maximum dose of 5 mg once daily. Amlodipine tablets can be taken with or without food, offering flexibility in administration, but it is generally advisable to take the medication at approximately the same time each day to maintain consistent drug levels in the body. If a dose is missed, it should be taken as soon as it is remembered, unless it is almost time for the next scheduled dose, in which case the missed dose should be skipped, and the regular dosing schedule resumed; patients should never double their dose to compensate for a missed one.

SIDE EFFECTS:
Like all medications, amlodipine can cause a range of side effects, varying in severity among individuals. Common mild to moderate side effects include peripheral edema, particularly swelling of the ankles and feet, headache, flushing, dizziness, fatigue, and nausea or abdominal pain. More moderate effects might encompass palpitations, somnolence, muscle cramps, and dyspepsia. Severe side effects, though less common, can include significant hypotension leading to fainting, severe allergic reactions characterized by rash, itching, swelling of the face, tongue, or throat, and difficulty breathing, which require immediate medical attention. In rare instances, patients may experience an increase in angina or myocardial infarction upon initiation or dosage increase, or develop gingival hyperplasia (gum overgrowth). Symptoms of an overdose typically involve profound and prolonged systemic hypotension, potentially leading to reflex tachycardia and, in severe cases, shock with a fatal outcome.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS:
Amlodipine is contraindicated in patients with known hypersensitivity to amlodipine or any other dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers. Caution should be exercised in patients with severe aortic stenosis, as the vasodilatory effects could potentially exacerbate their condition. Regarding pregnancy, amlodipine is classified as Pregnancy Category C, meaning animal studies have shown adverse effects on the fetus, but there are no adequate and well-controlled studies in pregnant women; it should only be used during pregnancy if the potential benefit justifies the potential risk to the fetus. Amlodipine is known to be excreted in human breast milk, so caution is advised when administered to a nursing mother, and the benefits of breastfeeding versus the potential risks to the infant should be carefully weighed. Significant drug-to-drug interactions can occur, particularly with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g., ketoconazole, ritonavir), which can increase amlodipine exposure, and CYP3A4 inducers (e.g., rifampin), which can decrease its effectiveness; co-administration with simvastatin requires a dose adjustment of simvastatin due to increased exposure. Alcohol consumption should be limited or avoided while taking amlodipine, as it can exacerbate the hypotensive effects and dizziness, increasing the risk of falls or syncope. Immediate emergency medical attention should be sought if a patient experiences severe chest pain, symptoms of an allergic reaction such as difficulty breathing or severe swelling, profound dizziness or fainting, or any signs of liver problems like yellowing of the skin or eyes.
