OVERVIEW

Domperidone is a peripheral dopamine D2 receptor antagonist that belongs to the class of medications known as prokinetic agents and antiemetics. It is primarily used to improve gastrointestinal motility and relieve nausea and vomiting by enhancing the movement of food through the digestive tract. Unlike many other dopamine antagonists, domperidone has limited ability to cross the blood brain barrier, which significantly reduces the risk of central nervous system side effects such as sedation and movement disorders. Its main mechanism of action involves blocking dopamine receptors in the gastrointestinal tract and in the chemoreceptor trigger zone located outside the blood brain barrier. Dopamine normally inhibits gastrointestinal muscle contractions, so blocking its action allows increased release of acetylcholine, resulting in stronger and more coordinated stomach and intestinal contractions. This helps accelerate gastric emptying and improves the movement of food from the stomach into the intestines. Domperidone also increases the pressure of the lower esophageal sphincter, reducing the likelihood of stomach contents flowing back into the esophagus. Because of these actions, it can help relieve symptoms such as bloating, fullness, nausea, and acid reflux associated with delayed gastric emptying. The medication is available in several dosage forms including oral tablets, orally disintegrating tablets, oral suspensions, and rectal suppositories in some countries. Domperidone is not approved for routine use in the United States because of concerns regarding serious cardiac side effects, although it is approved and commonly prescribed in many other countries under specific safety restrictions. The medication is generally intended for short term treatment at the lowest effective dose to minimize the risk of adverse events. Its effectiveness is greatest in conditions involving impaired gastric motility rather than disorders caused by excessive stomach acid alone. Because it acts mainly outside the central nervous system, domperidone is often preferred over certain other antiemetic medications in patients who are more susceptible to neurological side effects. The benefits and risks of treatment should always be carefully assessed before initiating therapy, especially in individuals with underlying heart disease or those taking medications that may increase its concentration in the body.

INDICATIONS & USES

Domperidone is primarily prescribed for the treatment of nausea and vomiting caused by various medical conditions or medications. It is commonly used to relieve symptoms associated with delayed gastric emptying, also known as gastroparesis, where food remains in the stomach longer than normal. Patients with diabetic gastroparesis may benefit from domperidone because it improves stomach contractions and accelerates gastric emptying. The medication is also used to reduce symptoms such as early satiety, bloating, abdominal discomfort, upper abdominal fullness, and post meal nausea associated with impaired gastric motility. In many countries, it is approved for the short term treatment of nausea and vomiting in adults and, under selected circumstances, in children. Some healthcare providers prescribe domperidone for gastroesophageal reflux disease when delayed gastric emptying contributes to reflux symptoms, although acid suppressing medications remain the primary treatment for reflux. It may also be used to relieve nausea associated with migraine attacks, allowing oral migraine medications to be absorbed more effectively. In patients receiving medications for Parkinson disease, domperidone is sometimes prescribed to reduce nausea caused by dopaminergic therapy without significantly interfering with the therapeutic effects of those medications in the brain. Off label, domperidone has been used to stimulate breast milk production in lactating women because dopamine blockade increases prolactin secretion, although this use remains controversial and requires careful medical supervision due to potential cardiac risks. Some specialists may prescribe it for functional dyspepsia characterized by upper abdominal discomfort, early fullness, and indigestion when impaired gastric motility is suspected. It has also been used in selected patients with chronic nausea related to gastrointestinal disorders after other treatment options have been considered. The decision to prescribe domperidone should take into account the patient's underlying medical condition, cardiac risk factors, and potential drug interactions. Treatment duration is generally kept as short as possible, and patients should be regularly reassessed to determine whether continued therapy is necessary.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION

The dosage of domperidone varies according to the patient's age, medical condition, and response to treatment. In adults, the usual starting dose is 10 milligrams taken orally three times daily before meals. Some patients may require an additional dose before bedtime if symptoms persist overnight, although total daily doses should generally remain within recommended safety limits. The maximum recommended daily dose in many clinical guidelines is 30 milligrams for most adults because higher doses are associated with an increased risk of serious cardiac arrhythmias. Domperidone is most effective when taken approximately 15 to 30 minutes before meals since food may delay its absorption and reduce the onset of action. Swallow tablets with water unless using an orally disintegrating formulation, which should be allowed to dissolve according to product instructions. Oral suspension formulations should be measured carefully using a calibrated dosing device rather than a household spoon to ensure accurate dosing. Elderly patients and individuals with liver impairment may require lower doses or avoidance of therapy because drug exposure may be increased. Patients with severe kidney impairment may require reduced dosing frequency because the medication is eliminated more slowly from the body. The duration of treatment is usually limited to the shortest period necessary to control symptoms, often not exceeding one week for acute nausea and vomiting unless otherwise directed by a healthcare professional. If a dose is missed, it should be taken as soon as remembered unless it is almost time for the next scheduled dose. Patients should not take two doses at the same time or increase the dose to compensate for a missed dose. Consistent adherence to the prescribed schedule helps maintain therapeutic effectiveness while minimizing adverse effects. Patients should never exceed the prescribed dose or continue treatment longer than recommended without consulting their healthcare provider, particularly because higher cumulative exposure increases the risk of potentially serious cardiac complications.

SIDE EFFECTS

Like all medications, domperidone may cause side effects, although many patients tolerate it well when used at recommended doses. Common mild side effects include dry mouth, headache, dizziness, mild abdominal cramps, diarrhea, constipation, and occasional nausea despite its antiemetic effects. Some individuals may experience fatigue or a general feeling of weakness during treatment. Because domperidone increases prolactin levels, hormonal side effects such as breast tenderness, breast enlargement, nipple pain, menstrual irregularities, decreased libido, or milk production in individuals who are not breastfeeding may occur. Men may rarely develop breast enlargement or tenderness due to elevated prolactin concentrations. Moderate side effects include persistent abdominal discomfort, skin rash, itching, palpitations, or episodes of lightheadedness that should prompt medical evaluation. Although neurological side effects are much less common than with medications that readily enter the brain, rare movement disorders such as muscle stiffness or involuntary movements have been reported, particularly in young children or susceptible individuals. The most serious adverse effects involve the cardiovascular system. Domperidone has been associated with prolongation of the QT interval on the electrocardiogram, which can lead to potentially life threatening ventricular arrhythmias including torsades de pointes. Patients may experience symptoms such as fainting, severe dizziness, rapid or irregular heartbeat, or sudden loss of consciousness if a dangerous heart rhythm develops. Severe allergic reactions are rare but may include swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat, difficulty breathing, widespread rash, or severe itching requiring immediate emergency care. Persistent chest pain, unexplained shortness of breath, or repeated episodes of syncope should also be treated as medical emergencies. Patients should promptly report any unusual or worsening symptoms to their healthcare provider so that the benefits and risks of continued treatment can be reassessed.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS

Domperidone should be used only when the expected benefits outweigh the potential risks, particularly because of its association with serious cardiac complications. The medication is contraindicated in patients with known prolonged QT interval, significant cardiac arrhythmias, moderate to severe heart failure, or conditions that substantially increase the risk of ventricular arrhythmias. It should also not be used in individuals with known hypersensitivity to domperidone or in patients with gastrointestinal bleeding, mechanical bowel obstruction, or gastrointestinal perforation where increased intestinal motility could be harmful. Caution is required in patients with liver disease because impaired hepatic metabolism may increase drug concentrations and the risk of adverse effects. Individuals with severe kidney impairment may require less frequent dosing due to reduced drug elimination. Domperidone should not be combined with medications that prolong the QT interval or strong inhibitors of the CYP3A4 enzyme, including certain macrolide antibiotics, azole antifungal agents, some antiviral medications, and several antiarrhythmic drugs, because these combinations significantly increase the risk of dangerous heart rhythm disturbances. Healthcare providers may recommend an electrocardiogram before and during treatment in patients with additional cardiac risk factors. Alcohol does not have a well established direct interaction with domperidone, but excessive alcohol consumption may worsen dizziness, gastrointestinal symptoms, and underlying medical conditions, so moderation or avoidance is generally recommended. During pregnancy, domperidone should be used only if the potential benefit justifies the potential risk to the developing fetus, and treatment decisions should be individualized by a qualified healthcare professional. Small amounts of the medication are excreted into breast milk, and although infant exposure is generally low, breastfeeding mothers should discuss the risks and benefits of treatment with their healthcare provider, especially when domperidone is being considered solely to increase milk production. Patients should seek immediate emergency medical attention if they develop chest pain, severe palpitations, fainting, sudden dizziness, difficulty breathing, swelling of the face or throat, or signs of a severe allergic reaction. They should also contact their healthcare provider promptly if nausea or vomiting persists despite treatment, if significant hormonal side effects develop, or if new symptoms suggest liver or heart problems. Regular review of all prescription medications, over the counter drugs, and herbal supplements is essential to reduce the risk of harmful drug interactions and ensure the safest possible use of domperidone.
