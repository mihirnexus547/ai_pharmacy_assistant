OVERVIEW

Metformin is an oral antihyperglycemic medication that belongs to the biguanide class of drugs and is considered the first-line treatment for most patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus. It has been used safely and effectively for several decades and is one of the most widely prescribed diabetes medications worldwide. Unlike insulin or certain other diabetes medicines, metformin does not directly stimulate the pancreas to release insulin. Instead, it primarily works by reducing the amount of glucose produced by the liver, increasing the sensitivity of body tissues to insulin, and improving the uptake and utilization of glucose by muscle cells. This combination of actions helps lower blood glucose levels without causing significant increases in insulin secretion. Because of its unique mechanism, metformin generally carries a much lower risk of causing hypoglycemia when used alone compared with insulin or insulin secretagogues. It also has beneficial effects on metabolism and may contribute to modest weight loss or weight neutrality, making it particularly suitable for overweight or obese individuals with type 2 diabetes. Metformin is absorbed through the gastrointestinal tract and is eliminated from the body primarily through the kidneys without undergoing significant metabolism in the liver. It is available in immediate-release and extended-release formulations, allowing healthcare providers to tailor therapy according to patient needs and tolerance. In addition to improving blood glucose control, long-term use of metformin has been associated with reductions in diabetes-related complications and may provide cardiovascular benefits in certain patients. The medication is commonly used alongside lifestyle interventions such as healthy eating, regular physical activity, and weight management to achieve optimal glycemic control. It can also be combined with many other classes of diabetes medications, including insulin, when additional blood glucose control is required. Because of its effectiveness, favorable safety profile, relatively low cost, and extensive clinical evidence, metformin remains a cornerstone of diabetes management across numerous international treatment guidelines. Healthcare providers generally recommend regular monitoring of kidney function, blood glucose, and long-term vitamin B12 levels during therapy to ensure continued safety and effectiveness.

INDICATIONS & USES

Metformin is primarily indicated for improving glycemic control in adults and children aged ten years and older with type 2 diabetes mellitus, particularly when lifestyle modifications alone are insufficient to maintain adequate blood glucose levels. It is considered the preferred first-line medication for newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes in the absence of contraindications because of its proven efficacy and long-term safety. The medication helps lower fasting blood glucose, postprandial blood glucose, and glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c), thereby reducing the risk of diabetes-related complications over time. Metformin is commonly prescribed as monotherapy early in the course of diabetes or in combination with other oral antidiabetic medications, injectable therapies, or insulin when additional glucose lowering is necessary. It is frequently recommended for overweight or obese individuals because it generally does not promote weight gain and may support modest weight reduction. In certain patients with prediabetes who are at particularly high risk of progressing to type 2 diabetes, metformin may be used to help delay or prevent disease progression alongside intensive lifestyle changes. One of the most common off-label uses of metformin is the management of polycystic ovary syndrome, where it may improve insulin resistance, restore menstrual regularity, promote ovulation, and enhance fertility in some individuals. It is also used off-label to address metabolic abnormalities associated with insulin resistance, particularly in patients with obesity or metabolic syndrome. Some healthcare providers prescribe metformin for weight management in carefully selected patients with insulin resistance, although it is not officially approved solely for weight loss. Ongoing research has explored its potential role in reducing cardiovascular risk, delaying age-related metabolic decline, and improving outcomes in certain chronic diseases, although these applications remain investigational and are not standard approved indications. In some specialized clinical settings, metformin has been evaluated for reducing the risk of gestational diabetes in high-risk populations, but insulin remains the preferred treatment when medication is required during pregnancy for diabetes control. The medication should always be prescribed based on an individual's overall health status, kidney function, and treatment goals. Its use should be accompanied by dietary counseling, regular physical activity, routine blood glucose monitoring, and periodic medical follow-up to maximize therapeutic benefits.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION

The appropriate dosage of metformin depends on the patient's age, kidney function, blood glucose levels, formulation being used, and overall response to treatment. For adults using immediate-release tablets, the typical starting dose is 500 mg taken twice daily with meals or 850 mg once daily with food. Starting with a lower dose helps reduce gastrointestinal side effects that commonly occur during the initial weeks of therapy. The dosage is usually increased gradually by 500 mg every one to two weeks based on blood glucose control and patient tolerance. Most patients achieve effective glucose control with total daily doses ranging from 1500 mg to 2000 mg, although some individuals may require up to the maximum recommended daily dose of 2550 mg for immediate-release formulations. For extended-release tablets, treatment commonly begins with 500 mg once daily taken with the evening meal, and the dose is increased gradually as needed. The maximum recommended daily dose for most extended-release formulations is 2000 mg, although certain products may allow slightly different maximum doses depending on manufacturer recommendations. Metformin should always be taken with food to minimize stomach upset, nausea, abdominal discomfort, and diarrhea. Tablets should be swallowed whole with adequate water, and extended-release tablets should not be crushed, split, or chewed because doing so may interfere with the controlled-release mechanism. If a dose is missed, the patient should take it as soon as remembered unless it is almost time for the next scheduled dose. If the next dose is due soon, the missed dose should be skipped and the regular dosing schedule resumed. Patients should never take two doses at the same time to compensate for a missed dose because this may increase the likelihood of adverse effects. Kidney function should be assessed before initiating therapy and monitored periodically throughout treatment since dosage adjustments or discontinuation may be necessary in patients with declining renal function. Patients should continue following dietary recommendations, exercise regularly, and attend scheduled medical appointments to ensure that treatment remains safe and effective.

SIDE EFFECTS

Metformin is generally well tolerated, but like all medications, it can cause side effects that vary in frequency and severity among individuals. The most common side effects involve the digestive system and are especially likely during the early stages of treatment or after dose increases. These commonly include nausea, diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal discomfort, stomach cramps, bloating, gas, indigestion, decreased appetite, and a metallic taste in the mouth. These gastrointestinal symptoms often improve as the body adapts to the medication or when the dose is increased gradually. Taking metformin with meals and using extended-release formulations can further reduce digestive side effects for many patients. Moderate side effects may include persistent gastrointestinal symptoms that interfere with daily activities, significant weight loss, fatigue, weakness, reduced appetite, or vitamin B12 deficiency after prolonged use. Long-term vitamin B12 deficiency may contribute to anemia, numbness, tingling sensations, balance problems, memory difficulties, or nerve damage if left untreated. Some patients may also experience mild skin reactions such as rash or itching, although these are relatively uncommon. Rare but serious adverse effects require immediate medical evaluation. The most important severe adverse reaction associated with metformin is lactic acidosis, a potentially life-threatening condition characterized by excessive accumulation of lactic acid in the bloodstream. Symptoms of lactic acidosis may include severe weakness, unusual muscle pain, difficulty breathing, extreme tiredness, dizziness, feeling cold, slow or irregular heartbeat, persistent vomiting, and severe abdominal pain. Although extremely rare, the risk increases in patients with severe kidney impairment, advanced liver disease, heavy alcohol consumption, dehydration, severe infections, or conditions causing reduced oxygen delivery to tissues. Severe allergic reactions, including swelling of the face or throat, difficulty breathing, and widespread rash, are also rare but require emergency medical treatment. Patients should promptly report persistent side effects, worsening symptoms, or any unusual changes in health to their healthcare provider so that appropriate adjustments to therapy can be made.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS

Metformin should only be used under the supervision of a qualified healthcare professional who can determine whether it is appropriate based on the patient's medical history and overall health status. The medication is contraindicated in patients with severe renal impairment because reduced kidney function significantly increases the risk of metformin accumulation and lactic acidosis. Kidney function should be evaluated before starting treatment and monitored regularly during therapy, especially in older adults or those with conditions that may affect renal function. Patients with acute dehydration, severe infections, shock, or conditions associated with poor oxygen delivery should temporarily discontinue metformin until the underlying illness has resolved and kidney function has stabilized. The medication should also be temporarily withheld before or at the time of certain imaging procedures involving iodinated contrast media in patients at increased risk of kidney injury, with resumption only after kidney function has been reassessed. Patients with severe liver disease should generally avoid metformin because impaired liver function may reduce the body's ability to clear lactate and increase the risk of lactic acidosis. Excessive alcohol consumption should be avoided because alcohol can potentiate the effects of metformin on lactate metabolism and increase the likelihood of serious complications. During pregnancy, insulin is generally considered the preferred treatment for diabetes requiring medication, although metformin may be used in selected circumstances when the benefits outweigh the potential risks under specialist supervision. Metformin passes into breast milk in small amounts, and breastfeeding decisions should be individualized after discussion with a healthcare provider, although it is generally considered compatible with breastfeeding in many situations. The medication may interact with certain drugs that affect kidney function, blood glucose control, or metformin elimination, making it important for patients to inform healthcare providers of all prescription medications, over-the-counter products, and herbal supplements they use. Long-term treatment may reduce vitamin B12 absorption, so periodic monitoring should be considered, particularly in patients with anemia or symptoms of neuropathy. Patients should seek immediate emergency medical attention if they develop symptoms suggestive of lactic acidosis, severe allergic reactions, chest pain, sudden difficulty breathing, loss of consciousness, or any other rapidly worsening or life-threatening symptoms. Regular medical follow-up, laboratory monitoring, adherence to prescribed dosing, and maintenance of a healthy lifestyle are essential to maximize the benefits of metformin while minimizing potential risks.
