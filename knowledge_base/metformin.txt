OVERVIEW:
Metformin is an oral antidiabetic medication belonging to the biguanide class, widely recognized as the first-line pharmacological treatment for type 2 diabetes mellitus. Its primary mechanism of action involves reducing hepatic glucose production, specifically by inhibiting gluconeogenesis, which is the liver's process of creating glucose. Additionally, metformin decreases the intestinal absorption of glucose from food and significantly improves insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues, thereby enhancing glucose uptake and utilization by muscle cells. Unlike some other antidiabetic drugs, metformin does not stimulate insulin secretion from the pancreas, which means it rarely causes hypoglycemia when used as monotherapy. This drug is characterized by its effectiveness in lowering blood glucose levels, its potential for modest weight loss or weight neutrality, and its established cardiovascular safety profile.

INDICATIONS & USES:
The primary and FDA-approved indication for Metformin is the management of type 2 diabetes mellitus in adults and children 10 years of age and older, typically used as an adjunct to diet and exercise. It is prescribed to improve glycemic control by lowering both basal and postprandial plasma glucose levels. Beyond its official approval, Metformin is commonly used off-label for several other conditions. One prominent off-label use is in the treatment of Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS), where it helps to improve insulin resistance, regulate menstrual cycles, and potentially aid in fertility. It is also sometimes used off-label for the prevention of type 2 diabetes in individuals at high risk, such as those with prediabetes, especially if they are obese or have a history of gestational diabetes. Furthermore, Metformin has been explored for its potential benefits in gestational diabetes, though its use in this context often falls under off-label or specialized guidelines depending on regional practices.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION:
Metformin is administered orally, typically starting with a low dose to minimize gastrointestinal side effects and gradually increasing to an effective maintenance dose. For adults, the usual initial dose is 500 mg once or twice daily with meals, or 850 mg once daily with the morning meal, with a maximum daily dose generally not exceeding 2000-2550 mg, divided into two or three doses. Pediatric patients (10-16 years old) usually start with 500 mg twice daily, with a maximum of 2000 mg per day. It is crucial to take Metformin with meals to reduce the incidence and severity of gastrointestinal upset. Extended-release formulations are available, which are typically taken once daily with the evening meal and should be swallowed whole without crushing or chewing. If a dose is missed, it should be taken as soon as remembered unless it is almost time for the next scheduled dose, in which case the missed dose should be skipped, and the regular dosing schedule resumed; never take a double dose to compensate for a missed one.

SIDE EFFECTS:
Metformin can cause a range of side effects, with gastrointestinal disturbances being the most common, especially during the initiation of therapy. Mild side effects often include nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, flatulence, and a metallic taste in the mouth, which usually subside over time or by taking the medication with food. Moderate side effects may include headache, dizziness, and fatigue. A rare but very serious side effect is lactic acidosis, characterized by symptoms such as unusual muscle pain, trouble breathing, stomach pain, dizziness, lightheadedness, an irregular heartbeat, and feeling cold, which requires immediate medical attention. Long-term use of Metformin can also lead to vitamin B12 deficiency, necessitating periodic monitoring. Symptoms of an allergic reaction, though uncommon, include rash, itching or swelling (especially of the face, tongue, or throat), severe dizziness, and trouble breathing. An overdose of Metformin can exacerbate gastrointestinal symptoms and significantly increase the risk of severe lactic acidosis, and may also cause hypoglycemia if taken with other glucose-lowering agents.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS:
Metformin is contraindicated in patients with severe renal impairment (eGFR < 30 mL/min/1.73m²), acute or chronic metabolic acidosis, including diabetic ketoacidosis, and in individuals with a known hypersensitivity to the drug. Conditions that predispose to lactic acidosis, such as congestive heart failure requiring pharmacological treatment, liver disease, acute myocardial infarction, severe infection, or dehydration, also represent significant contraindications. For pregnancy, Metformin is generally considered safe for use in gestational diabetes or pre-existing type 2 diabetes when the benefits outweigh the potential risks, but this decision should always be made in consultation with a healthcare provider. Small amounts of Metformin are excreted into breast milk, and while generally considered compatible with breastfeeding, infants should be monitored for any adverse effects. Several drug-to-drug interactions exist; for instance, cationic drugs eliminated by renal tubular secretion (e.g., cimetidine, ranitidine, amiloride, trimethoprim, vancomycin) can increase metformin concentrations. Furthermore, the use of iodinated contrast agents for radiological procedures can temporarily impair kidney function, increasing the risk of lactic acidosis, thus Metformin should be temporarily discontinued before and for 48 hours after such procedures. Alcohol consumption, especially excessive or binge drinking, significantly increases the risk of lactic acidosis due to its impact on lactate metabolism and can also cause hypoglycemia. Immediate emergency medical attention is warranted if symptoms of lactic acidosis, a severe allergic reaction, or severe hypoglycemia occur.
