OVERVIEW:
Azithromycin is a widely used broad-spectrum macrolide antibiotic known for its effectiveness against a variety of bacterial infections. It belongs to the azalide subclass of macrolide antibiotics, distinguished by its longer half-life and unique pharmacokinetic properties compared to other macrolides. The drug works by inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis; specifically, it binds to the 50S ribosomal subunit of susceptible bacteria, thereby blocking the translocation of peptides and preventing the growth and replication of the bacterial cells. While primarily bacteriostatic, meaning it stops bacteria from multiplying, it can be bactericidal at higher concentrations against certain organisms. Its primary characteristics include excellent tissue penetration, a prolonged post-antibiotic effect, and a convenient dosing regimen, often allowing for shorter treatment courses.

INDICATIONS & USES:
Azithromycin is prescribed for a wide range of bacterial infections affecting various body systems. Its official FDA-approved uses include the treatment of acute bacterial exacerbations of chronic bronchitis, community-acquired pneumonia, and pharyngitis/tonsillitis caused by susceptible organisms, particularly as an alternative for penicillin-allergic patients. It is also approved for uncomplicated skin and skin structure infections, acute otitis media, and sexually transmitted infections such as urethritis and cervicitis due to Chlamydia trachomatis, as well as chancroid. Common off-label uses extend to the prevention of Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC) disease in patients with advanced HIV infection, treatment of traveler's diarrhea, and as post-exposure prophylaxis for pertussis.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION:
Standard adult dosages of azithromycin vary significantly depending on the specific infection being treated. For respiratory tract infections, a common regimen involves a 500 mg dose on the first day, followed by 250 mg once daily for the next four days, often referred to as a "Z-Pak." For uncomplicated Chlamydia trachomatis infections, a single 1-gram oral dose is typically administered. Pediatric dosages are generally weight-based, such as 10 mg/kg on day 1 followed by 5 mg/kg on days 2-5 for conditions like acute otitis media. Azithromycin is available in oral forms, including tablets, capsules, and oral suspension, and also as an intravenous formulation for more severe infections or when oral administration is not feasible. It can generally be taken with or without food, though taking it with food may help reduce gastrointestinal upset for some individuals. If a dose is missed, it should be taken as soon as remembered unless it is almost time for the next scheduled dose, in which case the missed dose should be skipped to avoid doubling up.

SIDE EFFECTS:
Like all medications, azithromycin can cause a range of side effects, from mild to severe. Mild side effects commonly include gastrointestinal disturbances such as nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain, along with headaches. Moderate side effects may involve dizziness, skin rash, mild elevations in liver enzymes, and taste disturbances. More severe side effects, though less common, can be serious and include severe allergic reactions like anaphylaxis, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, or toxic epidermal necrolysis, which manifest as widespread skin blistering and peeling. Other severe risks include QT prolongation, which can lead to life-threatening heart rhythm abnormalities such as Torsades de Pointes, C. difficile-associated diarrhea (CDAD), and hepatotoxicity. Symptoms of an allergic reaction require immediate medical attention and may include hives, difficulty breathing, or swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat. In the event of an overdose, symptoms could include severe nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, hearing loss, and severe abdominal pain, necessitating urgent medical evaluation.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS:
Azithromycin is contraindicated in individuals with a known hypersensitivity to azithromycin, erythromycin, any macrolide or ketolide antibiotic, or any component of the formulation. It should also not be used in patients with a history of cholestatic jaundice or hepatic dysfunction associated with prior azithromycin use. For pregnant individuals, azithromycin is classified as Pregnancy Category B, meaning animal reproduction studies have shown no risk to the fetus, but there are no adequate and well-controlled studies in pregnant women; it should be used only if clearly needed. It is excreted in breast milk, so caution is advised during breastfeeding, and infants should be monitored for potential gastrointestinal upset. Significant drug-to-drug interactions can occur, including with antacids (which can reduce azithromycin absorption), warfarin (increasing the risk of bleeding), digoxin (potentially increasing digoxin levels), and other QT-prolonging drugs, which can heighten the risk of serious cardiac arrhythmias. While there is no direct interaction between azithromycin and alcohol, consuming alcohol during an infection may exacerbate gastrointestinal side effects and could potentially hinder the body's immune response. Immediate emergency medical attention should be sought if any signs of a severe allergic reaction, severe persistent diarrhea (especially if bloody or watery), yellowing of the skin or eyes, dark urine, severe dizziness, fainting, chest pain, irregular heartbeat, or any severe skin reaction develop.
