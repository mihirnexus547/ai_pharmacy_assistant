OVERVIEW

Azithromycin is a broad spectrum antibiotic that belongs to the macrolide class of antibacterial medications, specifically the azalide subclass. It is widely used to treat bacterial infections affecting various parts of the body and is valued for its convenient dosing schedule and prolonged duration of action. Unlike antibiotics that kill bacteria immediately, azithromycin primarily works by inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis, preventing bacteria from growing and multiplying so that the body's immune system can eliminate the infection. It achieves this by binding to the 50S ribosomal subunit of susceptible bacteria, thereby interfering with the production of essential proteins required for bacterial survival. Azithromycin has activity against many Gram positive bacteria, Gram negative bacteria, and atypical organisms such as Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Chlamydia pneumoniae, and Legionella pneumophila. One of its distinguishing characteristics is its excellent penetration into body tissues and immune cells, allowing it to reach higher concentrations in infected tissues than in the bloodstream. The drug also has a long elimination half life, which allows for once daily dosing and relatively short treatment courses compared with many other antibiotics. Azithromycin is available in multiple formulations including tablets, capsules, oral suspension, intravenous injection, and extended release oral suspension. It is generally well tolerated and has become one of the most commonly prescribed antibiotics worldwide because of its broad spectrum of activity and favorable safety profile. However, it is only effective against bacterial infections and has no activity against viral illnesses such as the common cold or influenza. Inappropriate or unnecessary use of azithromycin contributes to antibiotic resistance, making it important to use the medication only when prescribed for confirmed or strongly suspected bacterial infections. Healthcare providers often choose azithromycin because of its convenience, effectiveness against atypical pathogens, and reduced frequency of dosing, but careful patient selection remains essential due to potential cardiac and liver related risks.

INDICATIONS & USES

Azithromycin is approved for the treatment of a wide variety of bacterial infections involving the respiratory tract, skin, ears, and certain sexually transmitted infections. It is commonly prescribed for community acquired pneumonia caused by susceptible bacteria, including infections caused by atypical organisms such as Mycoplasma pneumoniae and Chlamydia pneumoniae. The medication is also approved for the treatment of acute bacterial sinusitis and acute bacterial exacerbations of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease when caused by susceptible bacteria. It is frequently used to treat streptococcal pharyngitis and tonsillitis in patients who cannot tolerate penicillin, although penicillin remains the preferred treatment for preventing rheumatic fever. Azithromycin is an effective treatment for acute otitis media, particularly in children, due to its convenient dosing schedule. It is indicated for uncomplicated skin and soft tissue infections caused by susceptible organisms. Another important approved use is the treatment of uncomplicated genital infections caused by Chlamydia trachomatis, where a single oral dose has historically been a common treatment option. It is also used in combination with other medications for the treatment and prevention of Mycobacterium avium complex infections in individuals with advanced HIV infection. Common off label uses include treatment of pertussis, also known as whooping cough, and certain cases of Lyme disease in patients who cannot take first line therapies. Physicians may prescribe azithromycin for traveler's diarrhea caused by susceptible bacteria, certain dental infections, and some gastrointestinal infections depending on local resistance patterns. It has also been used in selected patients with chronic inflammatory airway diseases because of its anti inflammatory properties, although this requires careful monitoring due to concerns about antibiotic resistance. During specific outbreaks or unusual infections, specialists may recommend azithromycin as part of combination therapy based on current clinical guidelines and susceptibility data. The choice to prescribe azithromycin should always consider the likely infecting organism, local antibiotic resistance patterns, patient specific factors, and current treatment recommendations.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION

The dosage of azithromycin depends on the type, severity, and location of the infection, as well as the patient's age, weight, kidney function, and liver function. For many common bacterial infections in adults, the standard regimen consists of 500 milligrams on the first day followed by 250 milligrams once daily on days two through five. In some infections, such as community acquired pneumonia or more severe bacterial illnesses, a healthcare provider may prescribe 500 milligrams once daily for three or more days depending on the clinical situation. Certain infections, including uncomplicated chlamydial infections, have traditionally been treated with a single oral dose of 1000 milligrams, although treatment recommendations may vary based on current guidelines and resistance patterns. Azithromycin should generally be taken at the same time each day to maintain consistent drug levels in the body. Tablets and oral suspension may usually be taken with or without food, although taking the medication with food may reduce stomach discomfort in some individuals. Extended release oral suspension should be taken on an empty stomach according to the specific product instructions. Patients should shake liquid formulations well before each use and measure doses using an appropriate measuring device rather than a household spoon. It is important to complete the entire prescribed course even if symptoms improve after a few days, because stopping treatment early may allow bacteria to survive and contribute to recurrent infection or antibiotic resistance. If a dose is missed, it should be taken as soon as it is remembered unless it is nearly time for the next scheduled dose. Patients should not take two doses at the same time to make up for a missed dose. Antacids containing aluminum or magnesium may reduce absorption of certain azithromycin formulations, so they should generally be taken several hours apart unless otherwise directed by a healthcare provider. Intravenous azithromycin is reserved for specific hospitalized patients and should be administered only under medical supervision. Dosage adjustments are not routinely required for mild to moderate kidney impairment, but caution may be necessary in patients with severe kidney or liver disease. Healthcare professionals should always individualize dosing based on the patient's overall clinical condition and current treatment guidelines.

SIDE EFFECTS

Like all medications, azithromycin may cause side effects, although many people tolerate the drug well without significant problems. The most common mild side effects involve the gastrointestinal system and include nausea, diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal pain, stomach cramps, indigestion, and loss of appetite. Some individuals may experience headache, dizziness, fatigue, or mild changes in taste during treatment. Mild skin rash or itching can occur in some patients and may resolve after the medication is discontinued. These common side effects are often temporary and usually do not require stopping treatment unless they become severe or persistent. Moderate side effects may include persistent diarrhea, significant abdominal pain, oral or vaginal yeast infections due to disruption of normal bacterial flora, and temporary elevations in liver enzymes detected on blood tests. Some patients may develop photosensitivity, making the skin more sensitive to sunlight, although this is relatively uncommon. Rare but serious side effects require immediate medical evaluation. Azithromycin can cause severe allergic reactions characterized by difficulty breathing, swelling of the face or throat, widespread rash, or hives. Severe skin reactions such as Stevens Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis, or drug reaction with eosinophilia and systemic symptoms are extremely rare but potentially life threatening. The medication has been associated with prolongation of the QT interval, which can lead to dangerous heart rhythm disturbances including torsades de pointes, particularly in susceptible individuals. Liver injury ranging from mild hepatitis to acute liver failure has been reported in rare cases and may present with yellowing of the skin or eyes, dark urine, severe fatigue, or abdominal pain. Antibiotic associated diarrhea caused by Clostridioides difficile can occur during treatment or even weeks after therapy has ended and may become severe if left untreated. Patients experiencing severe or persistent symptoms, chest pain, fainting, significant palpitations, or signs of a serious allergic reaction should seek immediate medical attention.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS

Azithromycin should only be used under the guidance of a qualified healthcare professional and only when there is a clear indication for antibiotic therapy. Patients with a known allergy to azithromycin, erythromycin, or other macrolide antibiotics should not take this medication because of the risk of serious allergic reactions. Individuals with a history of liver disease should use azithromycin cautiously, as the drug is primarily eliminated through the liver and has been associated with rare cases of severe liver injury. Patients with existing heart rhythm disorders, prolonged QT interval, low potassium or magnesium levels, or those taking medications that also prolong the QT interval require careful evaluation before starting treatment. Elderly patients and individuals with underlying cardiovascular disease may have a higher risk of cardiac complications. Azithromycin should be used cautiously in people with myasthenia gravis because it may worsen muscle weakness in susceptible individuals. During pregnancy, azithromycin is generally considered relatively safe when the potential benefits outweigh the risks, but treatment decisions should always be made in consultation with a healthcare provider. Small amounts of azithromycin pass into breast milk, and although it is generally considered compatible with breastfeeding, infants should be monitored for gastrointestinal disturbances or other unexpected effects. Alcohol does not have a direct major interaction with azithromycin, but excessive alcohol consumption may worsen stomach irritation, impair recovery from infection, and place additional stress on the liver. Drug interactions may occur with medications such as warfarin, digoxin, colchicine, certain antiarrhythmic agents, and other drugs that prolong the QT interval, making medication review important before starting therapy. Patients should inform their healthcare provider about all prescription medications, over the counter products, and herbal supplements they are taking. Emergency medical attention should be sought immediately if symptoms such as severe allergic reaction, difficulty breathing, fainting, severe chest pain, persistent palpitations, yellowing of the skin or eyes, severe or bloody diarrhea, or widespread blistering skin rash develop during or after treatment. Responsible use of azithromycin, adherence to prescribed dosing instructions, and prompt reporting of unusual symptoms help maximize treatment effectiveness while minimizing the risk of serious complications.
