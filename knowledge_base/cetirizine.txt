OVERVIEW:
Cetirizine is a second-generation antihistamine widely used for the relief of allergy symptoms. It belongs to the class of H1-receptor antagonists and is a piperazine derivative, known for its selective action on peripheral histamine receptors. The biological mechanism of action involves blocking the effects of histamine, a natural substance produced by the body during an allergic reaction, by preventing it from binding to H1 receptors. This action effectively reduces symptoms such as itching, sneezing, and runny nose without readily crossing the blood-brain barrier, which significantly minimizes the sedative effects commonly associated with older, first-generation antihistamines. Its primary characteristics include a rapid onset of action, providing relief typically within an hour, and a long duration of effect, offering 24-hour symptom control with a single daily dose.

INDICATIONS & USES:
Cetirizine is primarily indicated for the treatment of various allergic conditions affecting both adults and children. Its major medical conditions and symptoms it is prescribed for include seasonal allergic rhinitis, commonly known as hay fever, which manifests with sneezing, rhinorrhea, nasal pruritus, and ocular pruritus. It is also highly effective for perennial allergic rhinitis, which involves similar symptoms but occurs year-round due to indoor allergens. Furthermore, Cetirizine is officially FDA-approved for the treatment of chronic idiopathic urticaria, providing significant relief from the itching and rash associated with chronic hives of unknown cause. While its primary uses are well-defined, it is occasionally used off-label for other pruritic skin conditions or to manage allergic reactions to insect bites, though these are less common applications.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION:
The standard adult dosage for Cetirizine is typically 5 mg or 10 mg taken orally once daily, depending on the severity of symptoms and individual response. For pediatric patients, dosages vary by age: children 6 months to 2 years usually receive 2.5 mg once daily, potentially increasing to twice daily if needed; children 2 to 5 years typically take 2.5 mg once daily, with a maximum of 5 mg per day; and children 6 years and older can take 5 mg or 10 mg once daily. Cetirizine is available in various common routes of administration, including oral tablets, chewable tablets, and an oral solution or syrup, making it convenient for different age groups. It can be taken with or without food, but it is generally recommended to take it at the same time each day to maintain consistent drug levels. In the event of a missed dose, it should be taken as soon as remembered unless it is almost time for the next scheduled dose, in which case the missed dose should be skipped, and the regular dosing schedule resumed without doubling the dose.

SIDE EFFECTS:
While generally well-tolerated, Cetirizine can cause a range of side effects, from mild to, rarely, severe. Common mild side effects include drowsiness, although less pronounced than with first-generation antihistamines, dry mouth, fatigue, headache, dizziness, and nausea. Moderate side effects might involve increased drowsiness, blurred vision, stomach pain, or diarrhea. Severe side effects are rare but can include severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis), characterized by difficulty breathing or swelling, palpitations, very rare instances of liver problems, or urinary retention. Symptoms of an allergic reaction to Cetirizine include a rash, itching or swelling (especially of the face, tongue, or throat), severe dizziness, and trouble breathing, requiring immediate medical attention. An overdose may present with increased drowsiness, confusion, rapid heartbeat, tremors, or difficulty urinating; in children, agitation or restlessness may be more prominent.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS:
Cetirizine has several important warnings and precautions that users should be aware of. Key contraindications include a known hypersensitivity to cetirizine, hydroxyzine, or any other component of the formulation, as well as individuals with end-stage renal disease (creatinine clearance less than 10 mL/min). Regarding pregnancy and breastfeeding, Cetirizine is classified as Pregnancy Category B, meaning animal reproduction studies have shown no risk, but human studies are limited; it should only be used if clearly needed and the potential benefits outweigh the risks. It is excreted in breast milk, so caution is advised for breastfeeding mothers, and consultation with a healthcare provider is recommended. Drug-to-drug interactions can occur, particularly with central nervous system depressants like alcohol, sedatives, or tranquilizers, which can significantly increase drowsiness and impair judgment. While less common, ritonavir can increase cetirizine levels, and theophylline can slightly decrease cetirizine clearance. Alcohol consumption should be avoided or severely limited while taking Cetirizine due to the heightened risk of additive drowsiness and impaired mental alertness. Immediate emergency medical attention should be sought if symptoms of a severe allergic reaction, such as anaphylaxis, severe chest pain, profound difficulty breathing, severe dizziness or fainting, seizures, or signs of an overdose occur.
