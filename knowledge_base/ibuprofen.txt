OVERVIEW:
Ibuprofen is a widely used nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug, commonly known as an NSAID, available both over-the-counter and by prescription. It functions primarily by inhibiting the activity of cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, specifically COX-1 and COX-2, which are crucial for the synthesis of prostaglandins. Prostaglandins are lipid compounds that play a significant role in mediating pain, inflammation, and fever responses within the body. By blocking their production, ibuprofen effectively reduces these symptoms, providing relief from various discomforts. Its primary characteristics include its efficacy as an analgesic (pain reliever), antipyretic (fever reducer), and anti-inflammatory agent, making it a versatile medication for a broad range of conditions.

INDICATIONS & USES:
Ibuprofen is indicated for the relief of mild to moderate pain, encompassing a wide array of conditions such as headaches, dental pain, menstrual cramps (primary dysmenorrhea), muscle aches, and back pain. It is also highly effective in reducing fever associated with various illnesses. Officially, the FDA has approved ibuprofen for the treatment of pain, fever, and inflammation associated with inflammatory conditions like rheumatoid arthritis and osteoarthritis. Common off-label uses, though less frequent for ibuprofen compared to its approved indications, can include certain types of acute gout flares, although other NSAIDs might be preferred. Its broad spectrum of action makes it a go-to medication for many acute and chronic painful or inflammatory states.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION:
For adults, the standard over-the-counter dosage typically ranges from 200 mg to 400 mg taken orally every 4 to 6 hours as needed, not exceeding 1200 mg in a 24-hour period without medical supervision. Prescription dosages can be higher, up to 800 mg every 6 to 8 hours, with a maximum daily dose of 3200 mg under strict medical guidance. Pediatric dosages are weight-based, usually 5 to 10 mg/kg every 6 to 8 hours, and should not exceed 40 mg/kg/day. Ibuprofen is primarily administered orally in tablet, capsule, or liquid suspension forms, and it is generally recommended to take it with food or milk to minimize gastrointestinal upset. If a dose is missed, it should be taken as soon as remembered unless it is almost time for the next scheduled dose, in which case the missed dose should be skipped to avoid doubling up.

SIDE EFFECTS:
Ibuprofen can cause a range of side effects, from mild to severe. Common mild side effects include nausea, indigestion, heartburn, stomach pain, diarrhea, constipation, dizziness, and headache. Moderate side effects may involve the development of gastric ulcers, gastrointestinal bleeding (which can escalate to severe), tinnitus, and blurred vision. Severe side effects are less common but can be life-threatening and include severe gastrointestinal bleeding, acute kidney injury, liver dysfunction, severe allergic reactions such as anaphylaxis, and serious cardiovascular events like heart attack or stroke, particularly with prolonged high-dose use. Symptoms of an allergic reaction include hives, difficulty breathing, swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat, and a severe skin rash. An overdose may manifest as severe stomach pain, vomiting (potentially with blood), drowsiness, black or tarry stools, shallow breathing, fainting, or seizures.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS:
Ibuprofen is contraindicated in individuals with a known history of allergic reactions to NSAIDs or aspirin, active peptic ulcer disease, severe kidney or liver impairment, or severe heart failure. It should also be avoided in patients who have recently undergone coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery due to an increased risk of cardiovascular events. During pregnancy, ibuprofen should be avoided in the third trimester due to the risk of premature closure of the fetal ductus arteriosus and potential kidney problems in the fetus; use in the first and second trimesters should only occur if clearly necessary and under medical advice. While generally considered safe in low doses during breastfeeding, consultation with a healthcare provider is recommended. Ibuprofen can interact with various medications, including anticoagulants (e.g., warfarin), antiplatelet drugs (e.g., aspirin), corticosteroids, diuretics, ACE inhibitors, ARBs, lithium, and methotrexate, potentially increasing the risk of adverse effects. Concurrent alcohol consumption significantly increases the risk of gastrointestinal bleeding and may exacerbate liver damage. Immediate emergency medical attention should be sought if symptoms such as chest pain, sudden weakness on one side of the body, slurred speech, severe stomach pain, black or tarry stools, vomiting blood, signs of a severe allergic reaction, or significantly decreased urine output occur.
