Amoxicillin is a broad spectrum antibiotic that belongs to the aminopenicillin class of beta lactam antibiotics. It is widely prescribed to treat bacterial infections caused by susceptible organisms and has been in clinical use for several decades due to its proven effectiveness and favorable safety profile. Amoxicillin works by interfering with the formation of the bacterial cell wall, an essential structure that provides strength and protection to bacteria. Specifically, it binds to penicillin binding proteins within the bacterial cell wall, preventing the cross linking of peptidoglycan chains that are necessary for cell wall integrity. As a result, the bacterial cell wall becomes weak and unstable, eventually leading to bacterial cell rupture and death. Because of this mechanism, amoxicillin is considered a bactericidal antibiotic, meaning it kills bacteria rather than merely slowing their growth. It is effective against many Gram positive bacteria as well as selected Gram negative bacteria, making it useful for a wide range of infections. However, some bacteria produce enzymes known as beta lactamases that can break down amoxicillin and make it ineffective. In such cases, amoxicillin may be combined with clavulanic acid, a beta lactamase inhibitor, to overcome bacterial resistance. Amoxicillin is absorbed well after oral administration and achieves therapeutic concentrations in many tissues and body fluids. The medication is available in several formulations including capsules, tablets, chewable tablets, extended release tablets, and oral suspensions, allowing treatment to be tailored for both adults and children. It is generally well tolerated, relatively inexpensive, and recommended as a first line antibiotic for numerous common bacterial infections. Since amoxicillin is only effective against bacterial infections, it has no benefit in treating viral illnesses such as the common cold, influenza, or most sore throats caused by viruses. Responsible prescribing and adherence to antibiotic stewardship principles are important to reduce the development of antimicrobial resistance.

INDICATIONS & USES

Amoxicillin is prescribed for the treatment of numerous bacterial infections affecting different organ systems throughout the body. It is commonly used to treat infections of the ear, nose, and throat, including acute otitis media, bacterial sinusitis, streptococcal pharyngitis, and tonsillitis caused by susceptible bacteria. The medication is also widely used to manage lower respiratory tract infections such as community acquired pneumonia, acute bronchitis caused by susceptible organisms, and certain bacterial lung infections. Amoxicillin is frequently prescribed for urinary tract infections caused by susceptible bacteria, although resistance patterns may influence its use in some regions. It is effective in treating uncomplicated skin and soft tissue infections caused by sensitive bacterial species. Dentists often prescribe amoxicillin for dental abscesses, periodontal infections, and to prevent infective endocarditis in high risk patients undergoing specific dental procedures. The drug is an important component of combination therapy used to eradicate Helicobacter pylori infection in patients with peptic ulcer disease when used together with other medications such as proton pump inhibitors and additional antibiotics. Amoxicillin is also used to treat early Lyme disease caused by Borrelia burgdorferi in selected patients. In pediatric practice, it remains one of the most commonly prescribed antibiotics due to its effectiveness and favorable safety profile. FDA approved indications include infections of the ear, nose, throat, lower respiratory tract, urinary tract, skin, and infections caused by susceptible strains of bacteria. Common off label uses include treatment of selected bite wounds, certain uncomplicated gastrointestinal infections, and prophylaxis in specific medical situations where bacterial infection risk is elevated. Physicians may also prescribe amoxicillin for culture confirmed infections when laboratory testing demonstrates bacterial susceptibility. The choice of amoxicillin depends on local antibiotic resistance patterns, patient allergies, infection severity, and the specific bacteria suspected or confirmed to be causing the infection.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION

The dosage of amoxicillin depends on the patient's age, body weight, kidney function, type of infection, and severity of illness. In adults, common doses range from 250 mg to 500 mg every eight hours or 500 mg to 875 mg every twelve hours depending on the indication. More severe infections may require higher doses under medical supervision. Children generally receive weight based dosing, typically calculated in milligrams per kilogram of body weight each day and divided into two or three doses according to established treatment guidelines. Oral suspension formulations are commonly used in infants and young children to facilitate accurate dosing. Amoxicillin may be taken with or without food, although taking it with meals may reduce stomach upset in sensitive individuals. Patients should take the medication at evenly spaced intervals to maintain consistent antibiotic levels in the bloodstream throughout treatment. It is important to complete the full prescribed course even if symptoms improve before all doses are finished, as stopping treatment early may result in incomplete eradication of bacteria and contribute to antibiotic resistance. Adequate hydration should be maintained during therapy, especially in patients receiving higher doses. For extended release tablets, patients should swallow the tablets whole without crushing or chewing them and should follow the specific administration instructions provided with that formulation. If a dose is missed, it should generally be taken as soon as remembered unless it is almost time for the next scheduled dose. In that situation, the missed dose should be skipped and the regular dosing schedule resumed without taking a double dose. Patients with impaired kidney function often require dosage adjustments because amoxicillin is primarily eliminated through the kidneys. Healthcare providers may also modify treatment duration based on infection type, with some infections requiring only a few days of therapy while others may require treatment for ten to fourteen days or longer.

SIDE EFFECTS

Amoxicillin is generally well tolerated, but like all medications it may cause side effects that vary in severity among individuals. The most common mild side effects include nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, mild abdominal discomfort, bloating, and temporary changes in bowel habits. Some patients may experience headache, dizziness, or an unpleasant taste in the mouth during treatment. Mild skin rashes that are not allergic in nature may occur in certain individuals, particularly in children or patients with viral illnesses such as infectious mononucleosis. Oral or vaginal yeast infections may develop because antibiotics can alter the normal balance of microorganisms in the body. Moderate side effects may include persistent diarrhea, significant stomach pain, vomiting that interferes with hydration, or skin reactions that become widespread or increasingly uncomfortable. Some individuals may develop elevations in liver enzymes or temporary changes in blood cell counts that are usually detected through laboratory testing rather than symptoms. Rare but serious allergic reactions can occur, including anaphylaxis, which may present with difficulty breathing, swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat, severe itching, and widespread hives. Severe skin reactions such as Stevens Johnson syndrome or toxic epidermal necrolysis are extremely uncommon but require immediate medical attention. Antibiotic associated colitis caused by Clostridioides difficile may develop during treatment or even weeks after therapy has ended and can cause severe diarrhea, fever, and abdominal pain. Rare cases of liver injury, kidney inflammation, seizures in susceptible individuals receiving very high doses or with impaired kidney function, and severe blood disorders have also been reported. Patients should promptly report any persistent, worsening, or unusual symptoms to their healthcare provider for appropriate evaluation. Most mild side effects resolve after treatment is completed, but serious reactions require immediate discontinuation of the medication and urgent medical care.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS

Amoxicillin should not be used by individuals with a known allergy to amoxicillin, penicillin antibiotics, or other beta lactam antibiotics unless specifically advised by an allergy specialist. Patients with a history of severe allergic reactions to these medications are at increased risk of life threatening hypersensitivity reactions upon re exposure. Before starting therapy, healthcare providers should evaluate the patient's allergy history, kidney function, liver health, and previous antibiotic related adverse events. Individuals with infectious mononucleosis have a higher likelihood of developing a characteristic rash when treated with amoxicillin, so alternative therapies may be considered. Kidney impairment may require dosage adjustments because reduced elimination can increase drug levels and the risk of adverse effects. Although liver injury is uncommon, patients with existing liver disease should be monitored appropriately during prolonged treatment. Amoxicillin is generally considered safe during pregnancy when clinically indicated, and extensive clinical experience has not demonstrated an increased risk of major birth defects when used appropriately under medical supervision. Small amounts of amoxicillin pass into breast milk, but it is generally considered compatible with breastfeeding, although infants should be observed for diarrhea, rash, or oral yeast infection. Alcohol does not have a direct clinically significant interaction with amoxicillin, but excessive alcohol consumption may worsen gastrointestinal symptoms, impair recovery from infection, and reduce overall well being during treatment. Amoxicillin may interact with medications such as warfarin, methotrexate, probenecid, and allopurinol, potentially requiring closer monitoring or dosage adjustments. Certain hormonal contraceptives may theoretically be less reliable during episodes of severe vomiting or diarrhea caused by illness or medication, although routine reduction in contraceptive effectiveness from amoxicillin alone has not been consistently demonstrated. Patients should seek immediate emergency medical attention if they experience difficulty breathing, swelling of the face or throat, severe blistering skin reactions, persistent severe diarrhea with blood, seizures, or signs of a severe allergic reaction. Responsible use of amoxicillin only when prescribed for confirmed or strongly suspected bacterial infections is essential to preserve its effectiveness and reduce the growing problem of antibiotic resistance.
