OVERVIEW

Paracetamol, also known as acetaminophen, is one of the most widely used medications worldwide for the treatment of pain and fever. It belongs to the class of analgesic (pain-relieving) and antipyretic (fever-reducing) medicines. Unlike nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), paracetamol has very little anti-inflammatory activity and is therefore primarily used to relieve mild to moderate pain and reduce elevated body temperature. It is available over the counter in many countries and is also included in numerous prescription combination products containing opioids, cough suppressants, or cold and flu medications. Paracetamol has been used safely in clinical practice for several decades and is recommended as a first-line treatment for many common painful conditions. The exact mechanism by which paracetamol works is not completely understood, but it is believed to inhibit prostaglandin synthesis within the central nervous system by affecting cyclooxygenase enzymes, thereby reducing pain perception and lowering fever. It also acts on the hypothalamic heat-regulating center to promote heat loss and normalize body temperature during fever. Unlike NSAIDs, paracetamol has minimal effects on platelet function, gastric mucosa, and inflammation because its action is largely confined to the central nervous system rather than peripheral tissues. The medication is rapidly absorbed after oral administration, with peak blood concentrations typically occurring within thirty minutes to two hours depending on the formulation. It is extensively metabolized in the liver, primarily through glucuronidation and sulfation, with a small proportion converted into a toxic metabolite known as N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine (NAPQI). Under normal circumstances, NAPQI is detoxified by glutathione, but excessive doses can overwhelm this protective mechanism and cause severe liver injury. Paracetamol is available in tablets, capsules, oral liquids, dispersible tablets, suppositories, and intravenous formulations, making it suitable for patients of different ages and clinical situations. It is commonly used in adults, children, and older adults when taken according to recommended dosing guidelines. Because of its favorable safety profile at therapeutic doses, it is often preferred for patients who cannot tolerate NSAIDs due to stomach ulcers, bleeding disorders, or certain cardiovascular conditions.

INDICATIONS & USES

Paracetamol is primarily indicated for the relief of mild to moderate pain and the reduction of fever. It is commonly prescribed or recommended for headaches, including tension headaches, where it provides effective pain relief for many individuals. It is frequently used to treat toothaches, including pain before or after dental procedures. Patients experiencing muscle aches, sprains, strains, or minor sports injuries often use paracetamol to reduce discomfort. It is widely recommended for back pain, neck pain, and generalized musculoskeletal pain when inflammation is not the primary concern. The medication is commonly used to relieve pain associated with osteoarthritis and other chronic joint conditions, especially in patients who cannot take NSAIDs. It is also effective in reducing fever caused by viral infections such as the common cold, influenza, and COVID-19, as well as bacterial infections when appropriate antimicrobial therapy is also being administered. Paracetamol is frequently used after vaccinations to reduce fever and injection-site discomfort when clinically appropriate. It is commonly prescribed after surgical procedures to help control postoperative pain, either alone or in combination with stronger analgesics. The drug is often included in multimodal pain management strategies to reduce the need for opioid medications. In pediatric medicine, paracetamol is widely used for fever and pain relief in infants and children when dosed according to body weight. FDA-approved uses include the temporary relief of minor aches and pains and the reduction of fever. Common off-label uses include the management of migraine attacks as part of combination therapy, treatment of chronic pain conditions in selected patients, supportive care in palliative medicine, and pain management in hospitalized patients receiving intravenous therapy. It is also frequently combined with opioids such as codeine, tramadol, hydrocodone, or oxycodone to enhance pain control while allowing lower opioid doses. Healthcare providers may recommend paracetamol in patients with gastrointestinal conditions or bleeding risks where NSAIDs may be less suitable.

DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION

The appropriate dose of paracetamol depends on the patient's age, body weight, liver function, kidney function, and the specific formulation being used. For most healthy adults, the usual oral dose is 325 to 650 mg every four to six hours as needed for pain or fever, or 1000 mg every six to eight hours depending on the product instructions. The maximum recommended daily dose for most adults is generally 4000 mg from all sources combined, although many healthcare professionals recommend limiting routine use to no more than 3000 mg per day to reduce the risk of liver injury. Older adults or individuals with liver disease, chronic alcohol use, or poor nutritional status may require lower maximum daily doses. Pediatric dosing is based on body weight, typically 10 to 15 mg per kilogram every four to six hours as needed, with no more than five doses within twenty-four hours. Oral tablets and liquid formulations may be taken with or without food, as food has little effect on overall absorption, although taking the medication with food may help individuals who experience stomach discomfort. Extended-release formulations should be swallowed whole and should not be crushed, broken, or chewed unless specifically directed by the manufacturer. Intravenous paracetamol is administered by healthcare professionals in hospital settings according to body weight and clinical indication. Patients should carefully measure liquid formulations using a calibrated dosing device rather than a household spoon to ensure accurate dosing. It is important to read medication labels carefully because many combination cold, flu, and prescription pain medications also contain paracetamol, increasing the risk of accidental overdose. If a dose is missed and the medication is being taken on a regular schedule, it should be taken as soon as remembered unless it is almost time for the next scheduled dose. Patients should not double the next dose to make up for a missed one. The medication should be used at the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration necessary to control symptoms. Individuals should consult a healthcare professional if pain persists for more than several days or if fever continues despite appropriate dosing.

SIDE EFFECTS

Paracetamol is generally well tolerated when taken at recommended doses, and many people experience no significant side effects during short-term use. Mild side effects may include nausea, mild stomach discomfort, decreased appetite, headache, dizziness, or fatigue, although these occur relatively infrequently. Some individuals may experience mild skin irritation, itching, or rash as a result of hypersensitivity reactions. Moderate side effects can include persistent nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, unusual sweating, or mild elevations in liver enzyme levels detected during laboratory testing. Allergic reactions may present with hives, swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat, and difficulty breathing, requiring immediate medical evaluation. Rare but serious skin reactions such as Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis, and acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis have been reported and require immediate discontinuation of the medication and emergency medical care. The most serious adverse effect associated with paracetamol is liver toxicity resulting from overdose or prolonged excessive dosing. Symptoms of liver injury may include severe nausea, persistent vomiting, upper right abdominal pain, yellowing of the skin or eyes, dark urine, unusual fatigue, confusion, or easy bruising. Severe overdose can lead to acute liver failure, kidney injury, metabolic disturbances, coma, and death if not treated promptly. Chronic excessive use may increase the risk of kidney damage in susceptible individuals. Blood disorders such as thrombocytopenia, leukopenia, or pancytopenia have been reported rarely. Patients should seek urgent medical attention if they experience difficulty breathing, severe skin blistering, swelling of the face, confusion, seizures, loss of consciousness, or signs of liver failure. Prompt treatment with the antidote N-acetylcysteine can significantly reduce the risk of severe liver damage if an overdose is recognized early.

WARNINGS & PRECAUTIONS

Paracetamol should always be used according to recommended dosing instructions because exceeding the maximum daily dose significantly increases the risk of severe liver damage. Patients should carefully review all prescription and over-the-counter medications to avoid taking multiple products containing paracetamol at the same time. Individuals with known allergy or hypersensitivity to paracetamol should not use the medication. Patients with severe liver disease, active hepatitis, cirrhosis, or significantly impaired liver function should consult a healthcare provider before using paracetamol, and dose adjustments or alternative therapies may be necessary. Chronic alcohol consumption increases the risk of liver toxicity because alcohol affects liver metabolism and may reduce the body's ability to detoxify harmful metabolites. Patients are generally advised to avoid excessive alcohol intake while using paracetamol. Individuals with kidney disease should use the medication cautiously, particularly during prolonged therapy, and may require adjustments to dosing intervals. Paracetamol is generally considered one of the preferred pain relievers during pregnancy when clinically indicated and used at the lowest effective dose for the shortest necessary duration, although pregnant individuals should consult their healthcare provider before use. The medication is also considered compatible with breastfeeding because only very small amounts pass into breast milk and adverse effects in nursing infants are uncommon. Drug interactions may occur with medications such as warfarin, certain anticonvulsants, rifampin, isoniazid, and other drugs that affect liver enzyme activity, potentially increasing the risk of liver injury or altering anticoagulant effects. Patients with poor nutritional status or reduced glutathione stores may be more susceptible to toxicity even at relatively lower doses. Immediate emergency medical attention should be sought if an overdose is suspected, even if no symptoms are initially present, because serious liver damage may develop before noticeable symptoms appear. Emergency evaluation is also warranted for severe allergic reactions, extensive skin blistering, persistent vomiting, jaundice, confusion, difficulty breathing, seizures, loss of consciousness, or any signs suggesting acute liver failure. Regular consultation with a healthcare professional is recommended for individuals requiring long-term or frequent use of paracetamol, particularly those with underlying medical conditions or those taking multiple medications.
